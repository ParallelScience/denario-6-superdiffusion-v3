The current analysis successfully identifies the primary challenge: the system is in a pre-asymptotic, quenched-disorder-dominated regime that masks the theoretical Holtsmark limit. However, the interpretation of the results is currently hampered by the conflation of "trapping" with "ballistic motion" and an over-reliance on estimators (Hill, MLE) that are statistically underpowered for the available sample size.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **Misinterpretation of "Trapping":** You report $\beta_{trap} < 1$ at $N=40$ as "strong trapping," yet the MSD scaling is near-ballistic ($\alpha \approx 1.8$). In a quenched field, low-speed regions are not necessarily "traps" in the sense of Lévy walks; they are regions of low local velocity gradient. The current residence time analysis is likely measuring the time spent in low-velocity regions, which, in a quenched field, is a property of the spatial geometry, not a stochastic renewal process.
2. **Statistical Overreach:** The Hill and MLE estimators are being applied to datasets (5 tracers) that are far too small to resolve power-law tails. Reporting these values as "estimates" of $\alpha_{stable}$ or $\beta_{trap}$ is misleading. These should be treated as descriptive statistics of the specific realization, not as universal physical constants.
3. **VACF Interpretation:** The lack of a zero-crossing is the most important finding. It confirms that the system is not a Lévy walk. You should stop trying to force the vortex system into the Lévy walk framework and instead characterize it as a **persistent random walk in a correlated random field**.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Shift from Estimators to Scaling Collapses:** Instead of Hill/MLE estimators, use **Data Collapsing**. If the system is approaching a universal regime, the PDF of velocities $P(v; N)$ should collapse onto a master curve when normalized by a characteristic velocity scale $v_c(N)$. This is more robust than tail-fitting with $N=5$ tracers.
2. **Geometric Analysis over Stochastic Modeling:** Since the disorder is quenched, the "trapping" is purely spatial. Map the velocity field magnitude $|\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{r})|$ in 3D space. Identify the "null-velocity manifolds" (where filaments cancel out). The anomalous transport is likely driven by the topology of these manifolds. Future work should correlate tracer trajectories with the distance to the nearest null-velocity manifold rather than residence time thresholds.
3. **Abandon the Lévy Walk Comparison for Dynamics:** The Lévy walk is a poor proxy for quenched 3D vortex systems because it lacks spatial correlation. Use the Lévy walk only as a "null hypothesis" for the *shape* of the PDF, but acknowledge that the *dynamics* (VACF) will never match because the vortex system is non-ergodic and spatially correlated.
4. **Simplify the Reporting:** Stop reporting bootstrap CIs for $\alpha$ when $N=5$. It provides a false sense of precision. Instead, report the range of $\alpha$ across the 5 individual tracers to explicitly show the high variance caused by the quenched disorder.
5. **Future Iteration:** The next step should not be "more data" in the sense of more tracers, but rather **ensemble averaging over different filament realizations**. The current results are biased by the specific random placement of filaments. To get a true $\alpha_{stable}$, you need to average the MSD over many different spatial configurations of the $N$ filaments, not just different tracer starting positions within one configuration.
