## 3.1 Velocity Field Characterisation and Disorder Intensity

The instantaneous velocity vectors were computed via central differences on the noise-free (`x_true`, `y_true`, `z_true`) coordinates for each tracer, with boundary time steps discarded to avoid artefacts. The total velocity variance $\sigma_v^2(N) = \text{Var}(v_x) + \text{Var}(v_y) + \text{Var}(v_z)$ was computed by pooling all tracer velocity samples within each filament configuration.

The results reveal a strong dependence of disorder intensity on filament number. For $N = 5$ filaments, the speed range was extremely narrow, spanning only $[0.022, 0.042]$ m/s, indicating a nearly uniform, low-amplitude velocity field. As $N$ increases, both the mean speed and the speed range grow substantially: at $N = 10$ the range expands to $[0.060, 0.299]$ m/s, at $N = 20$ to $[0.063, 0.302]$ m/s, and at $N = 40$ to $[0.110, 0.884]$ m/s. The upper tail of the speed distribution grows by more than an order of magnitude from $N = 5$ to $N = 40$, consistent with the superposition of independent Biot-Savart contributions whose combined effect generates increasingly heavy-tailed velocity fluctuations. This behaviour is qualitatively consistent with the Holtsmark framework: as the number of independent filament sources increases, the velocity distribution should converge toward the Holtsmark stable law with stability index $\alpha_{\text{stable}} = 3/2$, which has a divergent variance. The finite speed range observed at all $N$ reflects the finite simulation domain and the quenched (fixed) disorder realisation rather than a true truncation of the distribution.

## 3.2 Anomalous Diffusion Exponents from 3D TAMSD

The time-averaged mean squared displacement (TAMSD) was computed for each individual tracer using the sliding-window formula $\overline{\delta^2}(\Delta t) = (T - \Delta t)^{-1} \sum_{t} [|\mathbf{r}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{r}(t)|^2]$, where $|\mathbf{r}|^2 = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ is the full 3D squared displacement. Ensemble averaging over the five tracers per $N$ configuration yielded $\langle \overline{\delta^2}(\Delta t) \rangle$. Power-law fits $\langle \overline{\delta^2} \rangle \sim \Delta t^\alpha$ were performed within a stable scaling window identified from the local log-log slope, and bootstrap confidence intervals (500 resamples at the tracer level) were computed for each $\alpha(N)$.

The fitted anomalous exponents are summarised in Table 1:

**Table 1.** Anomalous diffusion exponents $\alpha(N)$ for the 3D vortex filament system, with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals. The theoretical Holtsmark prediction is $\alpha = 1.5$.

| $N$ | $\alpha(N)$ | 95% CI |
|-----|-------------|--------|
| 5   | 2.000       | (1.999, 2.000) |
| 10  | 1.984       | (1.935, 1.996) |
| 20  | 1.978       | (1.939, 2.007) |
| 40  | 1.842       | (1.711, 1.957) |

Several features of these results merit careful discussion. First, all four configurations exhibit strongly superdiffusive transport with $\alpha > 1.5$, and the lowest-$N$ configurations ($N = 5, 10, 20$) are consistent with ballistic or near-ballistic scaling ($\alpha \approx 2$). This is physically interpretable: with few, fixed filaments, a tracer that happens to be placed near a filament experiences a nearly constant, strong velocity field for the duration of the simulation, producing approximately ballistic displacement. The quenched (frozen) disorder is critical here — because filament positions do not evolve, the velocity field experienced by a tracer is spatially heterogeneous but temporally persistent, leading to long-duration directed flights rather than the diffusive decorrelation that would arise from annealed (evolving) disorder.

Second, the most important trend is the systematic decrease of $\alpha$ with increasing $N$: from $\alpha \approx 2.00$ at $N = 5$ to $\alpha \approx 1.84$ at $N = 40$. This convergence toward lower values with increasing filament density is consistent with the theoretical expectation that, as $N \to \infty$, the velocity field approaches the Holtsmark distribution and the transport exponent should converge to $\alpha_{\text{diff}} = 1.5$. However, the convergence is slow, and even at $N = 40$ the measured exponent ($\alpha = 1.84$, 95% CI: $[1.71, 1.96]$) remains substantially above the Holtsmark prediction of 1.5. This indicates that the finite-$N$ regime studied here is still in a pre-asymptotic ballistic crossover, where individual filament encounters dominate the transport statistics.

Third, the bootstrap confidence intervals are notably wide for $N = 40$ (width $\approx 0.25$) and narrower for $N = 5$ (width $< 0.001$). The extreme narrowness at $N = 5$ reflects the near-perfect ballistic scaling rather than statistical precision — all five tracers exhibit essentially identical $\alpha \approx 2$ because the velocity field is nearly uniform. The wider intervals at $N = 40$ reflect genuine tracer-to-tracer variability in the heterogeneous multi-filament field. A critical limitation of the dataset is that only five tracers are available per $N$ configuration, which severely restricts the statistical power of the bootstrap. The reported confidence intervals should be interpreted as lower bounds on the true uncertainty; with $O(100)$ tracers, the intervals would be substantially narrower and the convergence trend more precisely resolved.

For comparison, the 3D isotropic Lévy walk ground truth yields the following fitted exponents: $\alpha = 1.61$ ($\beta = 1.2$, $\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.8$), $\alpha = 1.24$ ($\beta = 1.5$, $\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.5$), $\alpha = 1.25$ ($\beta = 1.8$, $\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.2$), and $\alpha = 1.06$ ($\beta = 2.5$, $\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.0$). The Lévy walk exponents are systematically lower than the vortex filament exponents at all $N$, confirming that the quenched vortex system is in a more strongly superdiffusive regime than the stochastic Lévy walk ground truth, even at the highest filament density studied. The $\beta = 1.5$ Lévy walk, which is the theoretical analogue of the Holtsmark-predicted transport ($\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.5$), yields $\alpha = 1.24$ in the finite-time simulation, illustrating that finite-time effects suppress the measured exponent below the asymptotic value even in the ground-truth system.

The MSD scaling curves and local slope panels are shown in the MSD scaling figure (`data/step_6_msd_scaling.png`), which displays log-log TAMSD versus lag time for all $N$ (vortex) and all $\beta$ (Lévy walk) configurations, together with local slope sub-panels that confirm the identification of the stable scaling windows.

## 3.3 Velocity Autocorrelation Function Analysis

The normalised 3D VACF $C_v(\Delta t) = \langle \mathbf{v}(t) \cdot \mathbf{v}(t + \Delta t) \rangle / \langle |\mathbf{v}(t)|^2 \rangle$ was computed per tracer using FFT-based autocorrelation and then ensemble-averaged across tracers for each $N$ configuration.

A striking result is that none of the four vortex filament configurations exhibit a zero-crossing of the VACF within the accessible lag-time window (up to $\Delta t \approx 6$ s, corresponding to half the total trajectory duration). The VACF remains positive throughout, indicating persistent velocity correlations that do not decay to zero on the simulation timescale. This is a direct consequence of the quenched disorder: because the filament positions are frozen, a tracer that enters a region of strong velocity field remains in that region for an extended period, maintaining a correlated velocity direction. The absence of a zero-crossing distinguishes the quenched vortex system fundamentally from both the annealed (evolving) vortex system and the Lévy walk ground truth.

In contrast, the Lévy walk VACF exhibits well-defined zero-crossings for $\beta = 1.5$, $1.8$, and $2.5$, at $\tau_c = 6.98$ s, $6.29$ s, and $5.68$ s respectively. The $\beta = 1.2$ Lévy walk also shows no zero-crossing within the accessible window, consistent with its strongly superdiffusive character ($\alpha_{\text{theory}} = 1.8$). The zero-crossing times for the Lévy walk decrease with increasing $\beta$ (decreasing superdiffusivity), reflecting the shorter characteristic flight durations at higher $\beta$. The physical mechanism generating velocity memory in the Lévy walk is fundamentally different from the vortex system: in the Lévy walk, memory arises from the temporal renewal structure of the flight-time distribution, whereas in the vortex system it arises from the spatial persistence of the frozen velocity field.

The absence of VACF zero-crossings in the vortex system, combined with the near-ballistic MSD scaling, provides a coherent picture: tracers in the quenched vortex field undergo extended directed flights whose duration is limited only by the spatial extent of the simulation domain, not by any intrinsic decorrelation timescale. This is qualitatively different from the Lévy walk scenario, where flights are terminated by a stochastic renewal event drawn from the Pareto distribution.

## 3.4 Velocity PDF Tail Analysis and Holtsmark Distribution Testing

The Hill estimator was applied to the empirical speed distributions $P(|\mathbf{v}|)$ for each $N$ configuration to estimate the tail stability index $\alpha_{\text{stable}}$. The Hill estimator $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{Hill}}(k) = k / \sum_{i=1}^{k} \ln(X_{(n-i+1)} / X_{(n-k)})$ was computed as a function of the number of upper-order statistics $k$, and the stable plateau region was identified to select the optimal $k$.

The results are summarised in Table 2:

**Table 2.** Hill estimator results for the speed distribution tail, compared to the theoretical Holtsmark prediction $\alpha_{\text{stable}} = 1.5$.

| $N$ | Optimal $k$ | $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{stable}}$ | Plateau $k$ range |
|-----|-------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| 5   | 897         | 24.76                         | [883, 912]        |
| 10  | 797         | 2.72                          | [783, 812]        |
| 20  | 466         | 5.57                          | [452, 481]        |
| 40  | 488         | 3.68                          | [474, 503]        |

The Hill estimates are substantially larger than the theoretical Holtsmark value of 1.5 for all $N$ configurations. At $N = 5$, the estimate of $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{stable}} \approx 24.8$ is consistent with a near-Gaussian velocity distribution, reflecting the extremely narrow speed range $[0.022, 0.042]$ m/s observed at this configuration — with only five filaments, the velocity field is nearly uniform and the speed distribution has a very thin tail. At $N = 10$, the estimate drops to $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{stable}} \approx 2.7$, indicating a heavier tail, and at $N = 40$ it reaches $\hat{\alpha}_{\text{stable}} \approx 3.7$. Notably, the Hill estimate at $N = 40$ is lower than at $N = 20$ ($3.7$ vs $5.6$), consistent with the expected trend toward heavier tails with increasing filament density, but neither value approaches the theoretical Holtsmark limit of 1.5.

These results indicate that the velocity PDF has not converged to the Holtsmark distribution at any of the $N$ values studied. The convergence is expected to be slow in $N$ because the Holtsmark distribution is the limiting distribution for an infinite random vortex gas, and the finite-$N$ corrections are substantial. The optimal $k$ values are large (ranging from 466 to 897, out of 2490 total samples), suggesting that the Hill estimator is identifying a plateau in the bulk of the distribution rather than the true asymptotic tail, which would require far more samples to resolve. The large optimal $k$ values also indicate that the speed distributions are not yet in the power-law tail regime at the sample sizes available, and the Hill estimates should be interpreted as upper bounds on the true tail index rather than reliable estimates of $\alpha_{\text{stable}}$.

## 3.5 Residence Time Distributions and Trapping Dynamics

Residence times were defined as the durations of contiguous intervals during which the tracer speed fell below the 25th percentile of the speed distribution for each $N$ configuration. The Pareto MLE estimator was applied to the pooled residence time durations to estimate the tail exponent $\beta_{\text{trap}}$ such that $P(\tau) \sim \tau^{-(1+\beta_{\text{trap}})}$.

The fitted residence time exponents are: $\beta_{\text{trap}} = 1.45$ ($N = 5$), $\beta_{	ext{trap}} = 4.61$ ($N = 10$), $\beta_{	ext{trap}} = 1.45$ ($N = 20$), and $\beta_{	ext{trap}} = 0.81$ ($N = 40$). The most physically significant result is the decrease of $\beta_{\text{trap}}$ from $N = 20$ to $N = 40$: at $N = 40$, $\beta_{\text{trap}} \approx 0.81 < 1$, which formally corresponds to a residence time distribution with a divergent mean, indicative of very strong trapping. This is consistent with the near-ballistic MSD scaling at $N = 40$: tracers that enter low-speed regions (near the null-velocity manifolds between filaments) can remain trapped for very long durations, contributing to the heavy tail of the displacement distribution.

The non-monotonic behaviour of $\beta_{\text{trap}}$ with $N$ (with $N = 10$ showing an anomalously high value of 4.61) likely reflects the small sample size (five tracers per $N$) and the sensitivity of the MLE estimator to the specific quenched disorder realisation. With only five tracers, the residence time statistics are dominated by the particular spatial arrangement of filaments encountered by each tracer, and a single realisation with few trapping events can produce an artificially high $\beta_{\text{trap}}$ estimate.

For the Lévy walk ground truth, the residence time exponents are $\beta_{\text{trap}} = 1.31$ ($\beta = 1.2$), $\beta_{\text{trap}} = 1.94$ ($\beta = 1.5$), $\beta_{\text{trap}} = 2.42$ ($\beta = 1.8$), and $\beta_{\text{trap}} = 2.92$ ($\beta = 2.5$). The Lévy walk residence time exponents increase monotonically with $\beta$, consistent with the shorter characteristic flight durations at higher $\beta$.
